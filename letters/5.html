Letter to Har Gopal Taftah: on publishing his Urdu Diwan

To: Har Gopal Taftah • Written in 1859

Original Urdu (خام خط)

میاں تفتہ! تمہارا خط اور دیوان کے چھپنے کی خوشخبری ملی۔ میاں، میرا دیوان چھپ رہا ہے، یہ سن کر خوشی تو ہوئی، مگر ڈر بھی لگتا ہے کہ کہیں کاپی نویسوں نے غلطیاں نہ کر دی ہوں۔ آج کل کے چھاپہ خانوں کا کچھ بھروسہ نہیں۔ کوئی لفظ غلط لکھ دیتے ہیں تو پورا مصرع بے معنی ہو جاتا ہے۔ میری التماس ہے کہ تم خود ایک بار پورے مسودے کو بغور دیکھ لو۔ اور ہاں، کتاب کا کاغذ اچھا ہونا چاہئے۔ اگر کاغذ خراب ہوا تو پڑھنے والے کا ذوق خراب ہو جاتا ہے۔ مجھے امید ہے کہ یہ دیوان جلد ہی لوگوں کے ہاتھوں میں ہوگا اور میری محنت تھیکانے لگے گی۔

Roman Transliteration

Mian Taftah! Tumhara khat aur deewan ke chhapne ki khushkhabari mili. Mian, mera deewan chhap raha hai, yeh sun kar khushi to hui, mager dar bhi lagta hai ke kahee copy-naveeson ne ghaltiyan na kar di hon. Aaj kal ke chhaapa-khaano ka kuch bharosa nahi. Koi lafz ghalt likh dete hain to poora misra be-maani ho jaata hai. Meri iltimaas hai ke tum khud ek baar poore maswade ko baghour dekh lo. Aur haan, kitaab ka kaaghaz accha hona chahiye. Agar kaaghaz kharaab hua to parhne wale ka zauq kharaab ho jaata hai. Mujhe umeed hai ke yeh deewan jald hi logon ke haathon mein hoga aur meri mehnat thikaane lagegi.

English Translation

My dear Taftah! I received your letter and the good news about the publication of my Diwan. Friend, hearing that my Diwan is being printed makes me happy, but I am also terrified that the copyists might have introduced errors. These days, printing presses cannot be trusted. If they write even one word incorrectly, the entire line loses its meaning. I request that you personally review the entire manuscript with great care. Also, the paper of the book must be of good quality. If the paper is poor, it ruins the reader's aesthetic pleasure. I hope this Diwan will soon be in the hands of readers, and my lifelong labor will bear fruit.

Literary & Historical Commentary

This letter details Ghalib's anxiety and meticulous attention to the publication of his Urdu Diwan (poetry collection). During the 19th century, printing technology was in its early stages in India, and lithographic printing heavily relied on scribes (copyists or 'katibs') to write the text onto plates. Scribes frequently made copying errors, which Ghalib feared would distort his poetic intentions.

Addressing Har Gopal Taftah, who was overseeing the printing process, Ghalib stresses the importance of proofreading. He notes that a single typo can ruin a 'misra' (line of poetry).

His emphasis on 'kaaghaz' (paper quality) highlights Ghalib's deep aesthetic sense. For Ghalib, bookmaking was an art form, and the physical feel and look of the book were essential to the overall literary experience.
