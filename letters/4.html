Letter to Alauddin Ahmad Khan Alai: on winter, warmth, and friendship

To: Alauddin Ahmad Khan Alai • Written in 1862

Original Urdu (خام خط)

علاؤ الدین خان بہادر! تمہارا خط آیا۔ میاں، یہاں دلی میں تو اب سخت سرما کا زور ہے۔ ہاتھ پاؤں شل ہوئے جاتے ہیں اور لکھنے کا حوصلہ نہیں پڑتا۔ تم نے مجھ سے میری صحت کا پوچھا۔ سچ تو یہ ہے کہ اب ضعیفی نے گھیر لیا ہے، صبح اٹھتا ہوں تو ہڈی ہڈی میں درد ہوتا ہے۔ بس ایک آتش دان کے سامنے بیٹھ کر وقت کاٹتا ہوں۔ مگر تمہارے خط کی گرمی نے میرے دل کو بھی گرما دیا۔ سچ کہتے ہیں کہ سچے دوست کی تحریر موسم سرما میں ایک گرم چادر کی طرح ہوتی ہے۔ دلی کے حالات بس ایسے ہی ہیں، کچھ خوشی نہیں, کچھ غم نہیں، زندگی گزر رہی ہے۔

Roman Transliteration

Alauddin Khan Bahadur! Tumhara khat aaya. Mian, yahan Dilli mein to ab sakht sarma ka zor hai. Haath paon shal hue jaate hain aur likhne ka hauslah nahi parta. Tum ne mujh se meri sehat ka poocha. Sach to yeh hai ke ab zaeefi ne gher liya hai, subah uthta hoon to haddi haddi mein dard hota hai. Bas ek aatish daan ke saamne baith kar waqt kaatta hoon. Magar tumhare khat ki garmi ne mere dil ko bhi garma diya. Sach kehte hain ke sacche dost ki tahreer mausam-e-sarma mein ek garam chaadar ki tarah hoti hai. Dilli ke haalat bas aise hi hain, kuch khushi nahi, kuch gham nahi, zindagi guzar rahi hai.

English Translation

My dear Alauddin Khan Bahadur! Your letter arrived. Friend, here in Delhi, the severe winter is in full force. My hands and feet feel completely numb, and I can barely find the courage to write. You asked about my health. The truth is that old age has completely taken over; when I wake up in the morning, every bone in my body aches. I do nothing but sit in front of a fireplace to pass the time. However, the warmth of your letter warmed my heart. They say truly that a true friend's letter is like a warm shawl in the winter season. The state of Delhi is just average—no great joy, no great sorrow, life simply passes by.

Literary & Historical Commentary

In this letter to his close friend and disciple, Alauddin Ahmad Khan 'Alai' (a nobleman and poet), Ghalib captures the physical reality of his later years in Delhi. He uses the metaphor of the harsh winter ('sarma') to reflect his own declining physical health and the cold social atmosphere of post-rebellion Delhi.

Despite his complaints about aging, Ghalib's signature wit shines through. The poetic comparison of a friend's letter to a 'warm shawl' ('garam chaadar') is an elegant example of Ghalib's conversational style, highlighting how he blended literary metaphors with everyday prose.

This correspondence shows the warmth Ghalib maintained in his personal relationships, which acted as his primary comfort during a period of financial hardship, isolation, and old age.
