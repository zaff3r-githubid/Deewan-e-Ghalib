Letter to Mir Mehdi Majrooh: the ruins of Delhi

To: Mir Mehdi Majrooh • Written in 1859

Original Urdu (خام خط)

میاں! دلی اب دلی کہاں ہے؟ اب تو یہ ایک چھاؤنی بن گئی ہے۔ نہ وہ قلعہ رہا، نہ وہ شاہجہان آباد، نہ وہ چاندنی چوک۔ انگریزوں نے شہر کو کھنڈر بنا دیا ہے۔ جہاں جاؤ، اداسی برستی ہے۔ میرے اتنے یار مٹ گئے، اتنے دوست جلاوطن ہو گئے کہ اب تنہائی کھائے جاتی ہے۔ کبھی کبھی لگتا ہے کہ میں مردوں کے شہر میں اکیلا زندہ رہ گیا ہوں۔ تم وہاں بیٹھ کر دلی کی خیر پوچھتے ہو، یہاں تو دل جل رہا ہے اور کوئی تسلی دینے والا نہیں رہا۔

Roman Transliteration

Mian! Dilli ab Dilli kahan hai? Ab to yeh ek chhaoni ban gayi hai. Na woh qila raha, na woh Shahjahanabad, na woh Chandni Chowk. Angrezon ne shehar ko khandar bana diya hai. Jahan jao, udaasi barasti hai. Mere itne yaar mit gaye, itne dost jalawatan ho gaye ke ab tanhai khaaye jaati hai. Kabhi kabhi lagta hai ke main murdon ke shehar mein akela zinda reh gaya hoon. Tum wahan baith kar Dilli ki khair poochhte ho, yahan to dil jal raha hai aur koi tasalli dene wala nahi raha.

English Translation

My dear friend! Where is Delhi now? It has become nothing but a military garrison. Neither does the Red Fort remain, nor Shahjahanabad, nor Chandni Chowk. The British have turned the city into a ruin. Wherever you go, melancholy reigns. So many of my dear companions have been wiped out, and so many friends exiled, that loneliness is now consuming me. Sometimes I feel as if I am the only living soul left in a city of the dead. You sit far away and ask me about the well-being of Delhi—here, my heart is burning, and there is no one left to console me.

Literary & Historical Commentary

This letter is a poignant historical document capturing the tragic aftermath of the 1857 Rebellion (the Mutiny) in Delhi. Following the British capture of Delhi, the city was subjected to massive destruction, looting, and executions. Muslims were temporarily banned from entering, and the cultural landscape was demolished.

Ghalib, who stayed in Delhi throughout the rebellion, watched his beloved city collapse. He mourns the loss of places like the Red Fort and Chandni Chowk, which were the epicenters of Mughal culture.

His prose here is deeply emotional, shifting from his typical humor to absolute grief. The sentence 'Dilli ab Dilli kahan hai' (Delhi is no longer Delhi) became a famous lament in Urdu literature for the end of the Mughal era.
